## Slide 2 — The Problem

**Headline:** 1.1 million international students. Nowhere to store their things.

**Bullets:**
- 1,126,690 international students enrolled in U.S. universities (IIE, 2024)
- Commercial storage averages $150/month — miles from campus
- No car, no truck, no local family — access is the real barrier
- Summer housing ends; belongings have no place to go

**Visual:** Split image — overloaded dorm hallway vs. a U-Haul truck far from campus (or simple text stat callout: **$150/month | Miles away | No vehicle**)

**Speaker notes:** "Every year, over a million international students face the same crisis — their lease ends in May but their flight home isn't until June, and they have no practical place to store three months' worth of belongings. Commercial storage exists, but it's expensive, it requires a vehicle, and it's designed for suburban homeowners, not carless college students."

## Slide 3 — Our Solution

**Headline:** .edu-verified peer storage, walking distance, no truck needed

**Bullets:**
- Students with unused space become Hosts and earn passive income
- Students needing storage become Renters — book online in minutes
- Both sides verified with `.edu` email — same campus community
- Flexible terms: 1 week to 3 months
- Physical handover happens on campus, on foot

**Visual:** Simple diagram — Renter icon ↔ Platform ↔ Host icon, with ".edu verified" badge on both sides

**Speaker notes:** "CampusShare connects these two groups that already live together. A Renter submits a booking request; a Host accepts. Both are verified members of the same campus. The item handover happens at a mutually agreed on-campus location — no truck, no drive, no storage facility account required."

## Slide 5 — Porter's Five Forces

**Headline:** Highest risk: substitutes. Biggest moat: .edu trust + network effects

**Bullets:**
- **Threat of New Entrants — Moderate:** low tech barrier, but `.edu` trust wall + review moat take time
- **Suppliers (Hosts) — Low–Moderate:** fragmented; review reputation creates soft lock-in
- **Buyers (Renters) — Moderate:** price-sensitive, but proximity advantage dampens switching
- **Substitutes — HIGH ⚠:** U-Haul, Public Storage, friend storage, shipping home
- **Rivalry — Low–Moderate:** no `.edu`-gated campus P2P storage competitor today

**Visual:** Porter's Five Forces radial diagram (Figure 3 / `porter_five_forces.png`) — Substitutes box in red

**Speaker notes:** "The most serious force is substitution — students have real alternatives like U-Haul or asking a friend. But those options require a vehicle, trust in a stranger, or large costs. No current competitor sits at the intersection of verified student identity, campus proximity, and short-term peer storage. That's our whitespace."

## Slide 6 — Generic Strategy

**Headline:** Focused Differentiation — not cheap, not broad, deeply trusted

**Bullets:**
- **Focus:** one campus, one verified community — not the whole storage market
- **Differentiation:** `.edu` identity + walking distance + photo documentation + dual reviews
- **Not Cost Leadership:** can't out-scale U-Haul on unit cost as a new entrant
- **Not Broad Differentiation:** expanding scope before product-market fit = stuck in the middle
- Porter (1985): attempting both without focus risks losing to everyone

**Visual:** 2×2 Porter strategy matrix with CampusShare dot in the Focused Differentiation quadrant

**Speaker notes:** "Porter warns against being 'stuck in the middle' — trying to be cheap and differentiated without focus. We've deliberately chosen the bottom-right quadrant: narrow scope, high differentiation. The `.edu` wall, the review system, the on-campus proximity — these create a value proposition that a general marketplace can't replicate and a big storage company has no reason to build."

## Slide 7 — Revenue Model

**Headline:** 10% commission + optional insurance — simple, aligned, scalable

**Bullets:**
- **Primary:** 10% platform commission on each completed booking (auto-deducted)
- **Secondary:** optional insurance add-on, $5–$15 per booking (tiered by declared item value)
- Airbnb comparison: host ~3% + guest 14–16% = far higher take rate (Airbnb, 2023)
- No subscriptions — conflicts with "save money" positioning
- No ads — user base too small at launch to monetize attention

**Visual:** Simple two-bar diagram: Primary revenue (commission) vs. Secondary revenue (insurance)

**Speaker notes:** "Revenue is simple and per-transaction. We take 10% when a booking completes — no payment until value is delivered. The insurance add-on is optional at checkout; it covers the Renter's declared item value for loss or damage. We deliberately excluded subscriptions and advertising because they'd either conflict with the student value prop or require scale we don't yet have."

## Slide 8 — Information Design (ERD)

**Headline:** 9 entities, 2 associative entities — Booking is the hub

**Bullets:**
- **Student** is one entity — same person can be Host or Renter
- **3 functional layers:** actor identity / core transactions / trust & risk
- **Booking** resolves Student (Renter) × StorageListing M:N — carries status lifecycle
- **Review** resolves mutual peer evaluation M:N — tied to verified Bookings
- Downstream entities: StoredItem, Payment, Dispute, InsurancePolicy all attach to Booking

**Visual:** ERD diagram (Figure 1 / `ERD.png`) — highlight Booking and Review boxes

**Speaker notes:** "The data model has nine entities organized in three layers. Everything flows through Booking — it's the central hub that connects the Renter, the Listing, and all downstream records. We modeled Host and Renter as roles of a single Student entity rather than separate entities, because the same person can flip between both roles over time. Booking and Review are associative entities — they resolve many-to-many relationships and carry their own attributes."

**Headline:** Every primary activity is scoped to the campus boundary — by design

## Slide 10 — Business Execution (BPMN)

**Headline:** 14 activities, 2 sub-processes, one complete storage transaction lifecycle

**Bullets:**
- **Stage 1 — Search & Select:** Renter logs in, `.edu` verified, queries listings, submits Booking
- **Stage 2 — Confirm:** Host approves/declines (XOR gateway); Renter pays; System generates agreement
- **Stage 3 — Handover:** both parties coordinate; System records `active` status
- **Stage 4 — Closeout:** return confirmed; mutual reviews written to Review table
- **Sub-processes:** Payment Processing (Stripe encapsulation) + Dispute Handling (exception path)

**Visual:** BPMN diagram (Figure 2 / `BPMN.png`) — point out the 3 pools and the two sub-process boxes

**Speaker notes:** "The BPMN shows the full booking lifecycle in BPMN 2.0 using a Database Interaction Layout. Every step that touches data has a dashed arrow connecting it to the correct database entity. The two sub-processes encapsulate complexity: Payment Processing hides the Stripe API interaction, and Dispute Handling isolates the exception path so it doesn't clutter the main success flow."

## Slide 11 — Ethics, Limitations & Future

**Headline:** Inference risk is real — and we've designed against it

**Bullets (Ethics):**
- Combined exposure risk: verified identity + Host address + item photos
- International students face higher barriers to law enforcement — disproportionate harm
- Mitigations: encrypted item photos (purged 90 days post-booking), address withheld until confirmation, campus drop-off option

**Bullets (Limitations):**
- Single-campus scope; sparse early reviews (bootstrapping problem); insurance workflow undefined; no admin entity in ERD; prohibited items not formally encoded

**Bullets (Future):**
- Cross-campus consortium; ML-based matching; dynamic pricing; logistics courier add-on; licensed insurance partnership

**Visual:** Three-column layout: Ethics | Limitations | Future

**Speaker notes:** "The most distinctive ethical risk isn't any single data point — it's the combination. Name plus address plus item photos creates a profile that could enable targeted theft, especially for international students who may struggle to engage local law enforcement. We've mitigated this by withholding addresses until a booking is confirmed and automatically purging item photos after 90 days. As for the future — once this model works on one campus, a cross-campus consortium is the natural next move."
